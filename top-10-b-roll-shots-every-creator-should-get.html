What Exactly is B-Roll?

In video production, your main footage—typically the primary subject, like someone talking to the camera—is called the "A-roll." B-roll is all the supplemental or alternative footage that is intercut with the main shot. It's the visual glue that holds your story together. Think of it as the cutaway shots, establishing scenes, and detailed close-ups that illustrate what the A-roll is talking about. Without it, videos can feel static and uninspired.

The Essential B-Roll Checklist: 10 Shots to Capture

Here is a step-by-step guide to the ten most crucial B-roll shots that will instantly improve your video quality.

  1. The Establishing Shot

    This wide or long shot establishes the scene and gives the viewer a sense of place. It answers the question, "Where are we?" Use it at the beginning of a video or a new scene to set the context, whether it's the outside of a building, a city skyline, or a wide view of a room.

  2. The Close-Up / Detail Shot

    Zoom in on the small details that matter. This could be a close-up of a person's hands typing, the steam rising from a coffee cup, or the texture of a product. These shots add sensory detail and emphasize key elements of your narrative.

  3. The Action Shot

    Show, don't just tell. Capture your subject (or yourself) in the process of doing something. If you're talking about painting, show the brush hitting the canvas. If it's a cooking video, show the chopping, stirring, and plating. Action shots make your content dynamic and prove your expertise.

  4. The Reaction Shot

    Capture the emotional response of a person to an event. A look of surprise, a smile of satisfaction, or a nod of understanding can be more powerful than words. This is crucial for interviews, vlogs, and narrative content to build an emotional connection with the audience.

  5. The Point-of-View (POV) Shot

    Put your audience directly into the action. A POV shot shows the world from the subject's perspective, like looking through the viewfinder of a camera or seeing their hands on a steering wheel. It's an immersive technique that makes the viewer feel like they are part of the story.

  6. The Landscape / Scenery Shot

    Even if your video isn't about travel, a beautiful shot of a landscape, a cityscape, or even just a compelling background can serve as a visual palette cleanser. It can be used to transition between topics or simply to add a moment of visual beauty.

  7. The Behind-the-Scenes (BTS) Shot

    Show your audience how the magic happens. BTS footage of your setup—your camera, lights, or microphone—builds authenticity and connection. It pulls back the curtain and makes your content feel more personal and transparent.

  8. The Slow Motion Shot

    Slowing down a moment can add drama, elegance, or emphasis. A splash of water, a celebratory confetti pop, or a dramatic hair flip all look incredible in slow motion. Use it sparingly to highlight a key moment and give it cinematic flair.

  9. The Timelapse / Hyperlapse

    Condense a long period into a few seconds. A timelapse is perfect for showing the passage of time, like clouds moving across the sky, a sunset, or a bustling street. A hyperlapse (a moving timelapse) adds an extra layer of dynamic energy, great for showing a journey or a process.

  10. The Textural / Abstract Shot

    This is a more artistic shot focused on colors, patterns, and textures. Think of light filtering through leaves, the grain of wood, or the bubbles in a drink. These shots add visual interest and can be used to create a specific mood or feeling without being literal.

Relevant Tools and Resources

While creativity is key, the right tools can make capturing B-roll much easier:

  • Camera: Any modern DSLR, mirrorless camera, or even smartphone can shoot excellent video.
  • Lenses: A versatile zoom lens (like a 24-70mm) and a prime lens (like a 50mm f/1.8) are great starting points.
  • Stabilizer: A tripod is essential for static shots like timelapses, while a gimbal is perfect for smooth, moving shots.
